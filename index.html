Fertility Treatments (IVF, ICSI, IUI)

🧬 In Vitro Fertilization (IVF)
Eggs retrieved after ovarian stimulation, fertilized in lab, embryos transferred to uterus. 5 steps: ovarian stimulation → egg retrieval → fertilization → embryo culture → transfer. Success: 40–50% under 35, declining with age.
Source: UCSF, MedlinePlus[reference:0][reference:1]
🔬 Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection (ICSI)
Single sperm injected directly into egg cytoplasm. Used for severe male factor infertility or failed IVF fertilization. Embryo then transferred.
Source: Penn Medicine[reference:2]
🫧 Intrauterine Insemination (IUI)
Washed sperm placed directly into uterus near ovulation. Less invasive, lower success (~10‑20% per cycle). Often first‑line for unexplained infertility, mild male factor.
📊 IVF Success Rates by Age
Under 35: 40–50%; 35–37: 30–40%; 38–40: 20–30%; Over 40: 10–20% per cycle. Donor eggs achieve 50–60%+.
Source: Fertility Philippines, Ovu[reference:3][reference:4]
Nursing role: Educate about medication side effects, manage OHSS risk, provide emotional support during cycles.

Advanced ART (GIFT, ZIFT, PGD, Donor)

🧬 GIFT (Gamete Intrafallopian Transfer)
Eggs + sperm placed into fallopian tube via laparoscopy. Fertilization occurs in vivo. Pregnancy rate ~25–30% per cycle.
Source: DeepDyve, PubMed[reference:5]
🔬 ZIFT (Zygote Intrafallopian Transfer)
Eggs fertilized in lab (IVF), then zygote transferred to tube. Combines IVF and tubal transfer; allows confirmation of fertilization before transfer.
Source: Healthwise[reference:6]
🧬 PGD (Preimplantation Genetic Diagnosis)
Embryo biopsy before transfer to screen for monogenic disorders, aneuploidy, or structural rearrangements. Raises ethical considerations (eugenics, embryo selection).
Source: ASRM, Bioethics Observatory[reference:7][reference:8]
💝 Donor Insemination / Egg / Embryo Donation
Donor sperm (AID) for male factor or same‑sex couples. Donor eggs for advanced age or ovarian failure (success 50–60%). Donated embryos (embryo adoption) from surplus IVF cycles, pregnancy rate ~49% per transfer.
Source: Invitra[reference:9], Adoptive Families[reference:10]
Note: In the Philippines, egg/sperm/embryo donation, PGT, and sex selection are not offered in most clinics; surrogacy and donor gametes face legal and ethical restrictions.
Source: Ovu Philippines[reference:11]

Surrogacy (Traditional & Gestational)

🤰 Traditional Surrogacy
Surrogate uses her own egg (genetic mother). Conception via IUI with intended father's or donor sperm. Greater legal complexity, higher ethical concerns (genetic tie).
Source: FIGO, Hatch[reference:12][reference:13]
👶 Gestational Surrogacy
Embryo created via IVF using intended parents' or donor gametes, transferred to surrogate (no genetic relation). Preferred method; clearer legal parentage.
Source: PMC, Hatch[reference:14]
  • Legal considerations: Gestational surrogacy generally favored; traditional surrogacy may lead to parental disputes. Some countries (France, Germany) prohibit surrogacy; others (US, UK) regulate.
    Source: FIGO, ScienceDirect[reference:15]
  • Nursing role: Provide non‑judgmental care, ensure informed consent, support all parties emotionally, and coordinate mental health screening.

Adoption & Fostering

📜 Domestic Adoption (Philippines)
Types: agency adoption, family/relative adoption (within 4th degree). Requirements: Filipino citizen ≥25, full civil capacity, income, 16‑year age gap (waivable for biological parents). Child must be <18 and declared available for adoption.
Source: DSWD, RA 8552[reference:16][reference:17]
🌍 Intercountry Adoption
Last resort when domestic adoption not possible. Governed by Hague Convention; requires adoption authority clearance.
Source: DSWD[reference:18]
🤝 Foster Care
Temporary placement of child in family home. Foster parents at least 16 years older than child, legal age, stable relationship, undergo training and licensing.
Source: DSWD, NACC[reference:19]
❤️ Nursing Role in Adoption/Fostering
Assess readiness of adoptive parents, provide education on child’s history and attachment, facilitate bonding, offer post‑placement support and referrals.
Key principle: “Preserving the child’s identity and culture” – domestic adoption preferred; intercountry as last resort.

Child‑Free Living & Counseling

Voluntary childlessness – the intentional decision not to have children despite biological capacity. Child‑free individuals identify this choice as central to their identity.

🌱 Reasons
Career aspirations, financial freedom, personal autonomy, environmental concerns, not seeing motherhood as appealing.
Source: AJOL, Nature[reference:20][reference:21]
💪 Outcomes
Higher marital satisfaction, financial stability, focus on personal growth, lower stress; may face social stigma (“selfish” label).
Source: Migration Letters[reference:22]
Nursing considerations: Respect patient autonomy, avoid assumptions about fertility desires, provide unbiased counseling, and explore all family‑building options (including child‑free) without pressure.
